# Biblical Archaeology — research for the "Artifacts" tab

Deep-research findings on whether a verse-anchored **Artifacts** layer (material
evidence: steles, reliefs, inscriptions, seals, coins, ossuaries) is feasible
for Scriptura's imagery pack, and what it would contain.

## Verdict: feasible, caption-driven, ~40–55 marquee pieces

It's viable and genuinely distinctive. The work is **not** image-sourcing
(images exist and are cleanly licensed) — it's **writing accurate, measured
captions**, because an artifact is meaningless without a sentence on what it is
and how it touches scripture. Scope is a *curated jewel-box* (dozens), not the
hundreds of an art tradition. Coins/seals/bullae could push it to ~70–100.

## Licensing map (the gating constraint — solved)

The key finding: **the holding museum's image policy doesn't matter**, because
the artifact is ancient (PD) and Wikimedia Commons carries **CC-BY-SA visitor
photographs** of the objects, which are the photographer's own work.

| Holder | Their own image licence | Route |
|---|---|---|
| **Met Museum** | **CC0** | Use directly (Assyrian reliefs, ANE) |
| **British Museum** | CC BY-NC-SA (NC = **blocked**) | Route around via Commons CC-BY photos of the same object |
| **Israel Museum / IAA** | Restrictive (paid hi-res) | Route around via Commons CC-BY photos |
| **Louvre / Cairo / Istanbul** | varies | Commons CC-BY photos |

Verified clean (non-NC) Commons images exist for: Tel Dan Stele (CC BY-SA 4.0),
Mesha Stele (CC BY-SA 4.0, + a YHWH-line close-up), Black Obelisk/Jehu (CC BY-SA
4.0), Cyrus Cylinder (CC BY-SA 4.0), Lachish Reliefs (CC BY-SA 4.0), Merneptah
Stele (CC BY-SA 4.0 + "Israel"-line close-up), Pilate Stone (CC BY-SA 2.0
original + CC BY 4.0 replica), Caiaphas Ossuary (CC BY-SA 3.0), tribute-penny
denarius (many CC-BY via the UK Portable Antiquities Scheme).

**Reliable systematic CC-BY-SA photographers** (sourcing shortcuts):
- **Osama Shukir Muhammed Amin FRCP** — ANE artifacts (BM, Iraq Museum, Sulaymaniyah), CC BY-SA 4.0, prolific & well-captioned.
- **Mike Peel**, **Carole Raddato** (Roman), **deror_avi** (Israel Museum), **Henri Sivonen** (Louvre).
Same per-plate licensing model as the stained glass (credit + licence in `attribution`).

## Scholarly backbone & TONE (the credibility issue)

- **Lawrence Mykytiuk, "53 People in the [Hebrew] Bible Confirmed Archaeologically"** (BAR 2014 + 3 more 2017) — the rigorous standard for inscriptions naming biblical persons; free PDF at Purdue (docs.lib.purdue.edu). He has a NT-figures companion too.
- **Wikipedia "List of artifacts significant to the Bible"** — comprehensive master list (used below).

**Tone rule (non-negotiable for a teacher-facing app):** captions state what the
object *attests* ("mentions the House of David", "names Pontius Pilate as prefect
of Judea"), never "proves the Bible." Measured, scholarly, no apologetic
overreach. This is the whole credibility of the feature.

## EXCLUDE — forgeries & disputed (credibility killers)

Omit, or include only with an explicit caveat: **James Ossuary** (suspected
forgery), **Jehoash Inscription** (suspected forgery), **Ivory Pomegranate**
(forgery suspected), **Shapira Scroll**, dubious seals (**Baruch**, **Ahaz**,
**Manasseh**), **Nazareth Inscription** (provenance disputed). Uzziah Tablet is
authentic but is a Second-Temple reburial plaque, not 8th-c. — caption accordingly.

## Caveats

- **Visual register is quieter than art.** A worn stone or small seal is less
  arresting than a Caravaggio — the *caption* carries the value. The tab reads as
  scholarly, not pretty; that's the point, but set expectations.
- **The real effort = captions** (accuracy + neutral tone), not fetching.
- **A few key objects share verses with art** (e.g. Yehohanan heel bone at John
  19:18, where the Crucifixion stack already lives) — but Artifacts is its **own
  tab**, so no collision with the Art expander.

## Data-model fit

Slots onto the existing `imagery` schema with **no new columns** — the `caption`
field (currently unused by the art layers) is exactly where the explanatory
sentence goes. New `kind`/`tradition` value (e.g. `kind='artifact'`), a new
`source='artifact'`, and a third reader tab "Artifacts" alongside Art/Where. The
`art_for`-style lookup is identical (book + loc range), just filtered to the
artifact kind for the new tab.

# Expansion research: everyday material culture (2026-06-03)

The current roster (46) is heavy on **royal inscriptions and monuments** — the
glamorous 5% that *name* people. It answers "did these kings exist?" far better
than "what was life like?" This section curates **everyday material culture** to
add the texture of the world the Bible describes: writing, worship, farming,
trade, home. These illuminate a *practice* rather than a single name, but they
still verse-link cleanly (a lamp → Matt 5:15, a figurine → Jer 7:18).

**Tone/curation unchanged:** measured; non-NC images only (PD/CC0/CC-BY);
forgeries and high-doubt objects excluded. Note the recurring temptation here —
**unprovenanced market pieces** (many seals/bullae) — which now also fails the
new provenance test, so prefer *excavated* examples.

**Sourcing reality (good news for this category):** the **Metropolitan Museum
(CC0)** holds a deep, freely-usable trove of ANE everyday objects — pottery,
lamps, figurines, weights, ivories — photographed on clean backgrounds. Plus
**Gary Todd (CC0)** and **Osama S. M. Amin (CC-BY-SA)** for museum pieces, and
Commons visitor photos for in-situ installations. This is *easier* sourcing than
the monuments were.

## Design fit & open question

These slot into the existing TOML/bridge with **no schema change** — same `entry`
shape, with the new `provenance` field doing real work here (excavated vs market).
The one design question is **placement**: everyday objects don't belong to a
single reign, so either (a) interleave them into the existing era chapters by
date, or (b) add one cross-cutting chapter — *"Everyday life in the biblical
world"* — grouping home, work, and worship. (b) reads more coherently and keeps
the era spine about politics/events; (a) keeps strict chronology. **Lean (b).**

Realistic first batch (the HIGH table) = **~8 objects**, mostly Met CC0, low
fetch-risk. That alone would rebalance the gallery from "epigraphy" toward
"archaeology."
